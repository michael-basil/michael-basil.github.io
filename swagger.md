When I was 13 years old, I was the leading scorer, assist leader, and offensive driver of our soccer teams. I took all the penalty kicks. I never asked for this or demanded it, rather it was directed to me. I never missed. Until one day I did. I hit the post. The ball bounced back to me and I wound up to kick and hesitated and stopped. I was about to break a rule on the field. I looked around and saw that my teammates didn't know what to do to support me. This was confusing. They didn't step up. They were just waiting for me to score even after the miss, not able to process that I was not allowed to touch the ball until another player did. I don't remember if we won or lost that game, but I am glad that it happened in retrospect.

The next time a penalty shot opportunity came up I was directed to take it again. I aimed just inside the post, essentially trying to sneak it in so the goalie wouldn't have a chance to block it.  The ball came off my foot exactly as intended, BUT I hit the post squarely. I can still visualize it.  If I was aiming for the center of the post I couldn't have been more accurate. It was a thoroughly planned, precisely executed, yet beautiful miss. I don't remember if we won or lost that game, but it was hard at the time and I am glad that it happened in retrospect.

After that game, I asked the coach to direct someone else to take the kicks while I figured out what was going on with me. I recall he was a bit puzzled at the time, but he thought about it for a minute and then agreed. Shortly after that, at the next game with a penalty shot conversion opportunity, he chose Brian, one of our defenders on the field at the time. Brian readily accepted the opportunity, seemingly somewhat puzzled for a fleeting moment as he jogged up the field toward the PK circle. After a moment of hesitation and thought, I jogged back to cover his position in case a counter-attack resulted - **_we don’t leave unprotected knowable attack vectors._** Brian scored easily with a less precise approach than mine.

Vito, our coach, would frequently drive me and another player with an overly-busy single mother, to and from practice and games. In the car ride home after that game, I asked Vito how he knew Brian would score. He told me that he didn't know, but had noticed multiple times that Brian had been practicing penalty shots in his backyard and figured he would give us a high probability of penalty shot conversion, which is all that he could control.

_Making a high quality decision is the best thing any coach or commander can do. In this instance, Brian showed Merit & Affinity and the Commander made the switch with a decisive intentional action while simultaneously Coaching both Brian and Michael individually._

I recall that we won that game and that I was relieved to have more time to process, happy for Brian’s newfound success, the victorious result for the team, and glad my performance overall was unaffected. Any penalty shot issues were localized, like a basketball player that cannot temporarily do well with free throws.

Later that season another opportunity came up for a penalty kick in a key situation. I had been taken out on the play on a breakaway from behind. As I got up, I realized that I didn’t have the same negative energy about the pending PK as before. I wasn’t sure what I felt, but it was different and the appropriate time to request another attempt. When Vito directed Brian to come up to make the attempt, I met Brian halfway and stopped to chat with him saying "Brian, I'd like to take this kick if it is okay with you". He said ... "Sure! Go for it." and instinctively ran back to cover his position as Left Defender.

I had no plan until I walked up to the ball other than to do something closer to what Brian would do because he seemed to understand something I had not yet learned. I stayed in the present moment and aimed into what I perceived as my strength and his weakness - essentially mimicking Brian. I was going to shoot right, which is his left. I aimed for the fluorescent green and neon pink logo on the left pinky of his goalkeeper gloves, with a bias to skew right towards the corresponding post. I recall a sudden rush of immediate fear conflated with the thought, “what if he gets a hand on it?” My value judgement was to mitigate the risk by shooting with max power and immediate post-kick crash to capitalize on a potential rebound. In my estimation based on the power and placement the worst likely case was a hard direct rebound at a 45 degree angle.

The whistle blew (meaning the goalie can move and so could I). He hopped a bit perhaps to try and throw me off. I didn't worry about what he was going to do. His attempt to block the kick was delayed and unsuccessful. I will never forget the visualization of the ball hitting the back part of the netting at the seam. I had set up like a Skilled Archer, something that I learned from one of our Defenders. I resolved that I had been making myself miss by trying to be **_Avoidant & Perfect instead of Democratic & Powerful_**. At thefollowing practice, Brian and I decided to form a PK and set-play committee approach which paid dividends down the road in various unexpected ways.

We won the rest of our games that season and the division. Vito never made another command directive until next pre-season when we had become soft and slow.

I learned various lessons that year through coaching and peer modeling from Vito, Brian, and many others, but it was Vito with his trademark Italian eyes and smirk that would be the first to tell you that Swagger is a good thing, always with a wink ...

**_“When you’re great at something, it’s virtuous to let people know about it!”_**

**_- Vito the Coach in the early to mid 1990s_**
